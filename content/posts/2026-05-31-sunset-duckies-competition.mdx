Noah was weirdly quiet in the car.

Not sulking quiet. Not tired quiet. The other one, where you can tell the brain is running too fast and the mouth has decided not to get involved.

The board was finished. It had survived the shaping bay, the respirators, the spraying, the glassing, the decals, the adults touching the rail and nodding in that grave adult way. It looked like a surfboard. It looked, if I let myself say it, pretty damn good.

But on land everything gets to lie a little.

A board can look fast on a rack and feel dead under your feet. It can have the right dimensions and still paddle like a door. It can carry three months of work and one very confident duck, then meet the first little Tamarin wave and immediately reveal that everyone has been admiring an object, not a surfboard.

So we took it to the water with other kids watching. Because apparently parenting is mostly inventing ways to make your own heart rate worse.

## A Very Serious Contest, Obviously

The format was tiny. A few heats. Two or three kids in the water. Best two waves counted, if the adults remembered to count. Someone had a whistle. Someone had a clipboard. The whole operation had the kind of authority you get from a gazebo and a folding table, which is exactly the right amount of authority for eight-year-old surfing.

I didn't want a big event. I wanted just enough structure that the kids treated it like a thing, and just little enough structure that nobody forgot this was still a bunch of children at Tamarin trying to catch waves.

<ImageFloat src="/images/board-project/IMG_4994.jpeg" alt="Noah in a dust mask during the build, the look he had before paddling out" side="right" width="40%">

This photo is from the build, not the contest, but it has the look I saw again that morning: slightly serious, slightly pretending not to be serious, fully aware that adults have now made the thing real.

</ImageFloat>

The other kids knew the board before it hit the beach. They had seen drawings. They had voted on duck versions. They had argued about colours with the confidence of people who have never paid an invoice. The board was not a reveal. It was the finished version of a thing they had already been allowed to care about.

That made the first paddle-out worse and better at the same time.

Noah didn't give a speech about pressure. He just carried the board differently. Less like a toy, more like something that could embarrass him.

## The First Wave

I wish I had a perfect photo of the first wave. I don't. I was too busy doing the useless parent thing where you stand on the beach and try to control physics with your face.

He paddled. The board moved. Not beautifully, not cinematically, not like a surf movie. It moved enough.

Then the wave picked him up and the whole project changed category.

Until that second, the board was a workshop story. Foam dust, sanding, paint, stickers, Daniel and the Santosha guys doing their magic, Noah learning not to rush. After that wave, it was just his board.

The important technical review, from the beach:

- [x] It paddles.
- [x] It catches small Tamarin waves.
- [x] It turns when he asks, at least enough for an eight-year-old to feel it.
- [x] It did not become an expensive wall decoration with fins.

## The Sand Part

The day had a second half on land. Stickers, a few tees, some caps, a folding table next to the cooler. Mostly free. I kept the commerce brain out of it because the duck did not need a funnel. It needed to escape the board.

<ImageFloat src="/images/board-project/IMG_8757.jpeg" alt="The family at home with Noah's red board and the finished Sunset Duckies board on the rug" side="left" width="44%">

The funny thing about stickers is that they make ownership fuzzy in a good way. A board belongs to one kid. A sticker can jump to another board, a bottle, a laptop, a cap. It lets the project spread without asking anyone to take it too seriously.

</ImageFloat>

That was the part I didn't expect to care about. Noah made the first mark, but the other kids helped choose what it became. When they wore it, or stuck it on something, they weren't being customers. They were saying, in kid language: yes, this is ours too.

By the end of the morning the board had done the only two jobs I really cared about. It surfed. And it gave a few kids the feeling that they were in on something together.

That's a lot for one duck.
