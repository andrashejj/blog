The board was finished, but that did not mean it was real.

On land, a surfboard can get away with a lot. It can be beautiful and useless. It can look fast on a rack and feel dead under your feet. The only honest test is water, and the harder version of that test is water with other kids watching.

So the first session became a small contest at Tamarin. Nothing grand: a few heats, the same kids who are always around the break, one gazebo, a cooler, and a folding table with stickers on it.

## The format

The format was deliberately thin. Twenty-minute heats. Two or three kids in the water. An adult on the sand with a whistle and a clipboard, performing the necessary theatre of order.

Best two waves counted, more or less. The real question was simpler: could they paddle out, wait their turn, commit to a wave, and come back in feeling like they had done something?

<ImageFloat src="/images/board-project/IMG_4994.jpeg" alt="Noah in a dust mask during the build, the look he had before paddling out" side="right" width="40%">

The bracket did not matter much. What mattered was that everyone knew which board this was. They had seen the drawings, voted on logo versions, argued about colours, and watched some of the sanding. The duck was not a surprise reveal. It was an object that had slowly become familiar before it ever touched the water.

</ImageFloat>

Noah was nervous in a way I had not seen during the build. Sanding foam is private. This was not. If the board surfed badly, it would not fail in a workshop. It would fail in front of the exact people he wanted to impress.

He did not say any of this. He just got quieter in the car.

The board surfs.

That is the whole headline. A board shaped partly by an eight-year-old had every chance of becoming a lovely object that did not work. This one works. It is loose enough, fast enough for the wave, and it turns when he asks it to.

## The merch table

The other half of the day happened on the sand. We had printed stickers, a few tees, and some caps, and put them on the folding table next to the cooler. Mostly free. There was no commercial plan hiding underneath it.

Still, it changed the day. A project that had started at our kitchen table and then passed through the hands and opinions of the other kids was suddenly walking around on them. That is a strange thing to watch as a parent. Your child makes a mark, other children help shape it, and then they choose to wear it.
