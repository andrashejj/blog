Almost twenty years ago, I caught a lucky break and spent a few months working in San Francisco. I was very junior, wide-eyed, and didn’t know much about anything. A local Managing Director handed me a book that had nothing to do with my job: **The Unknown Craftsman** by Soetsu Yanagi, adapted by Bernard Leach.

I didn’t read it immediately. But when I finally opened it, the ideas inside rewired how I looked at the world.

The book serves as the manifesto for the Mingei (folkcraft) movement. It celebrates simple forms, honest materials, and everyday objects created entirely without ego. Yanagi and Leach return constantly to one central idea: *“objects born, not made.”* These are things that emerge organically from repetition, care, and necessity—not from a desperate attempt to impress anyone. 

(If you want to go deeper into Japanese craft philosophy, there’s an excellent reading list on [Sentomono's blog](https://www.sentomono.com/blog/5-books-about-japanese-craft).)

I think about this concept often. I see it in software, in engineering, and in visual design. The most elegant code I’ve ever encountered possesses this exact quality. It isn't flashy. It isn't clever just for the sake of being clever. It simply feels inevitable, as if it couldn't possibly have been written any other way.

<Divider />

This philosophy came rushing back to me recently, brought on by my eight-year-old son, Noah. 

His birthday is coming up. He’s been eyeing my fitness watch for weeks and recently declared that he desperately needs one, too.

Fair enough. But the request rubbed me the wrong way. I pictured him with another screen strapped to his wrist, buzzing with notifications, counting his steps for a week before the novelty wore off. It felt like introducing him to another object optimized for metrics rather than meaning. 

I didn't want him tracking his physical life on a screen. I wanted him to actually experience it. So, I tried to redirect his attention toward something tangible. 

It’s a long story, but after a lot of talking, we traded the idea of a smartwatch for a custom surfboard from the local legends at [Santosha Surf](https://santoshasurf.com/).

{/* photo: the Santosha shaping bay, or Noah looking at boards */}

A few days ago, we walked into their shaping bay. Instead of just ordering a board, I asked the shapers if we could be involved in the process—if Noah and I could actually learn how to shape it ourselves. They paused. They told us no one had ever asked them that before, though they’d often thought about offering an apprenticeship-style experience. 

So, just like that, we have a project. 

The rough outline looks like this:

- Learn the fundamentals — materials, tools, foam blanks, templates.
- Document every session — photos, notes, and the inevitable mistakes.
- Understand the shaper’s eye — how they manage to "see" the finished board trapped inside a raw block of foam.
- Write about it here, and maybe film parts of the process.

{/* photo: boards in different stages of shaping, or foam blanks stacked */}

I spend most of my life working in software—staring at screens, wrangling abstractions, building things you can’t actually touch. The shaping bay is the absolute antithesis of that. It’s foam dust hanging thick in the air. It’s the mechanical scream of a planer chewing through a blank. It’s one of the guys stepping back, squinting at the foam under angled lights, and explaining why a specific rail needs to be an eighth of an inch thinner to handle how the wave breaks at our local beach.

It’s a highly specific, deeply physical, hard-earned kind of knowledge. I haven’t been around anything quite like it in a long time.

More than anything, though, I just want to build something real with my son. Something we shape with our own hands, cover in foam dust, and eventually carry into the ocean together.
