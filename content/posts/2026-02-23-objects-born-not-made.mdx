Almost 20 years ago I got lucky and spent a few months working in San Francisco. I was very junior, did not know much about anything. The local MD recommended me a book. **The Unknown Craftsman** by Soetsu Yanagi, adapted by Bernard Leach.

I did not read it right away. But when I did, it stuck.

The book is basically the manifesto behind the Mingei movement. Simple aesthetics, basic form, the value of handmade objects. Yanagi and his long-time friend Bernard Leach talk about "objects born, not made." Things that come from repetition and care, not from trying to impress.

If you want to go deeper into Japanese craft philosophy, there is a nice reading list on [Sentomono's blog](https://www.sentomono.com/blog/5-books-about-japanese-craft).

I still think about this. In software, in engineering, in more visual design work. The best code I have seen has that same quality. Not clever, not showy. Just works. Born, not forced.

<Divider />

This came back recently in a very different way.

My son Noah wanted a fitness watch. Fair enough. But something about it bugged me. Another screen on his wrist, buzzing, tracking, giving him little hits of dopamine for doing nothing. So instead of buying the thing, I tried to redirect him toward actual fitness.

Long story. But we ended up agreeing that he will get a custom surfboard from [Santosha Surf](https://santoshasurf.com/).

{/* photo: the Santosha shaping bay, or Noah looking at boards */}

We met with the shapers a few days ago. Very cool guys. We asked them if we could be involved in the process, learn the craft of shaping. They said they had never done this before but had been thinking about it and thought it was a great idea.

So now we have a project. I am still figuring out the details, but roughly:

- learn the fundamentals — materials, tools, foam blanks, templates
- document every session — photos, notes, what went wrong
- understand the shaper's eye — how they see a board inside a blank
- write about it here, maybe film some of it too

{/* photo: boards in different stages of shaping, or foam blanks stacked */}

I spend most of my time in software. I build products, I write code, I think in systems.

But standing in that shaping bay, watching people who read waves and turn them into curves with their hands — I had not seen anything like it in a long time. Yanagi would have liked it.

I want Noah and me to build something together. Not just a board. Something where nobody looked at a screen the entire time.

More updates to come.
