The dangerous part of cleaning up a child's drawing is that you can fix it to death.

Noah drew the duck in pencil, quickly, at the kitchen table. It had the confidence of a thing that did not know it would later be inspected by adults with software.

The finished logo was less solo than that sounds. Noah gave us the duck and the attitude. Then the other kids voted on versions, changed their minds, defended bad options, and brought back details I had removed too early. My job was to make it printable, maskable, sprayable, and still theirs.

<ImageFloat src="/images/board-project/IMG_4982.jpeg" alt="Noah's original pencil sketch: two board views labelled Back side, Logo, Pad, Lightning, with the note Sunset Ducky designed by Noah" side="right" width="44%">

The sketch had more information in it than it first seemed. A circle near the nose with an arrow pointing to it: "Logo." A grid at the tail: "Pad." A bolt: "Lightning." Along the rail: "Sunset Ducky, designed by Noah."

The work was translation, then negotiation.

</ImageFloat>

## The mascot

The duck does most of the work because it should not work at all. It is a bath toy with sunglasses and a backwards cap, riding a wave on a shortboard. On paper that sounds like a bad sticker from a tourist shop. In Noah's drawing it had just enough arrogance to survive.

Behind it sits a sunset, banded orange to yellow in a tight circle. That is where half the name comes from. The other half is the duck, because children are often better at naming things than adults. They stop before they ruin it.

The exact duck changed a lot. Some versions were too polished. Some were too babyish. Some looked like clip art. The useful test was whether the kids still reacted before I explained anything.

## The wordmark, the tagline, the line

Under the logo, the name is set in a heavy condensed sans, all caps, stacked. It is not subtle. It should not be. The whole thing is going on a child's surfboard, not above the door of a quiet hotel.

Below it, in a loose handwritten script, is "main character energy." I would not have written that line. That is why it stayed. It belongs to the internet Noah and the other kids are growing up inside, not to me.

The tagline lives on the bottom of the board:

"No cap" means no lie. It also, on a board with a duck wearing a cap, has the kind of stupid second meaning that is only available if you do not overthink it. Noah liked that. So did I.

## The palette

Three colours do most of the work: warm cream, teal, and the orange-yellow sunset. We tried other things. We put options next to each other and asked the kids. They were blunt in the way only children are blunt. The versions that needed explaining died quickly.

That was the pattern. More polished usually meant worse. Moving back toward the first drawing usually fixed it.

## From one board to everything

Once the artwork existed, it started showing up elsewhere. A traction pad. Stickers. A few tees and caps. This was not a strategy. It was a consequence. If you give a group of kids a duck logo they helped choose and access to printing, the duck will not stay in one place.

<ImageFloat src="/images/board-project/IMG_5644.jpeg" alt="Noah applying the big Sunset Duckies sticker to the finished board at home, fins stacked beside him" side="left" width="44%">

The work, on my end, was restraint disguised as design. Make it printable. Make it maskable. Make it clean enough to survive on foam under gloss. But do not make it sensible, and do not overrule the weird little preferences that made the kids point at one version and not another. Sensible would have killed it.

</ImageFloat>

The flames never happened, which was probably the best design decision in the whole project. It wasn't mine. The finished duck isn't purely Noah's either. He started it. The others pulled it into shape by caring enough to argue. It lives at [sunsetduckies.com](https://sunsetduckies.com) now, which still feels slightly ridiculous. Good.
