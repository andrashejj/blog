I keep having the same conversation with myself, usually on a train, usually after a week where one project made money and another made my brain light up.

Do I take the boring work or the fancy work?

Early in my career I pretended the answer was obvious. Pick the hard thing. Pick the thing with the steepest learning curve. Pick the thing that sounds good when you explain it over dinner. Money mattered, but I acted as if admitting that made the work smaller.

I was wrong about that.

The older I get, the more I respect boring work that pays, compounds, and leaves enough energy for the rest of life. A healthy business with clear economics and decent people is not a consolation prize. It is a very underrated object.

Right now I have both extremes on my plate. At Motif we're building an independent wealth advisor that works only for the client. Some of the problems are pure R&D: you solve one hard thing and two new unknowns walk in behind it. In parallel I have a tech leadership mandate at MCO (My Compliance Office), which by startup stereotype is the boring one, except the business performs, the people execute, and the economics are real.

The fancy work teaches me fast and drains me faster. The boring work steadies me, then tempts me to coast. Neither mode is morally superior. They just charge different prices.

My current rule:

- [ ] Keep one thing that stretches me.
- [ ] Keep one thing that pays without eating the whole week.
- [ ] Don't confuse adrenaline with meaning.
- [ ] Don't confuse stability with sleepwalking.

Some weeks Motif gets the extra push. Some weeks the grown-up mandate gets the best hours because that's where the pressure belongs. The trick is noticing which mode I'm in before I start lying to myself about why.
