I keep having the same argument with myself on trains. One project made money that week. The other made my brain light up. By the second stop I have usually invented a moral reason to prefer whichever one I already wanted.

Do I take the boring work or the fancy work?

Early in my career I pretended the answer was obvious: pick the hard thing with the steepest learning curve, preferably the one that sounds good over dinner. Money mattered, but I acted as if admitting that made the work smaller.

I was wrong about that.

The older I get, the more I respect work that pays, compounds, and leaves enough energy for the rest of life. A healthy business with clear economics and decent people is a strangely underrated object.

Right now I have both extremes on my plate. At Motif we're building an independent wealth advisor that works only for the client. Some of the problems are pure R&D: you solve one hard thing and two new unknowns walk in behind it. In parallel I have a tech leadership mandate at MCO (My Compliance Office), which by startup stereotype is the boring one, except the business performs, the people execute, and the economics are real.

Motif teaches me fast and drains me faster. MCO steadies me, then tempts me to coast. The mistake is treating either feeling as evidence that the work is morally better. They're invoices in different currencies.

For now, I keep both:

- [ ] Keep one thing that stretches me.
- [ ] Keep one thing that pays without eating the whole week.
- [ ] Don't confuse adrenaline with meaning.
- [ ] Don't confuse stability with sleepwalking.

Some weeks Motif gets the extra push. Some weeks MCO gets the best hours because that's where the pressure belongs. I still have the argument on the train. I just trust the moral reasons less now.
