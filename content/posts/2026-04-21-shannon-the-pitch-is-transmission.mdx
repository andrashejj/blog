Shannon's [*A Mathematical Theory of Communication*](https://people.math.harvard.edu/~ctm/home/text/others/shannon/entropy/entropy.pdf) (1948) is the paper that gave us the bit, entropy, and channel capacity. On the surface it's about telegraphs and telephones. Underneath it's a theory of what it even means to transmit something from one mind to another through a noisy medium — which is exactly what sales and fundraising are.

A few of the claims map almost too cleanly.

## Information is selection

Shannon's opening move is to strip out meaning entirely: the engineering problem is that one message gets selected from a set the receiver was already entertaining, and information is how much that selection narrows the possibility space.

For sales this is unforgiving. If a wealth-platform CIO hears "AI-powered financial intelligence" and files it under "another AI vendor," you've transmitted zero bits. You have to land inside a set they're actively considering — analyst-capacity problem, personalization at scale, compliant advice automation — and then narrow it.

Your 30-second pitch ("personalized investment advice at scale, without hiring more analysts") does this well. Longer decks tend to drift back toward the high-probability strings that carry no information.

## Entropy is surprise-per-symbol

A sentence like "we're AI-powered, building the future of finance" has near-zero entropy for a fintech investor because every pitch says it. A sentence like "10 signed LOIs representing 500K+ end-users, Liminal-backed, SIX as brokerage partner, SRO coverage across FX/commodities/digital assets" has high entropy — very few companies can emit that sequence.

<Callout type="info" title="Rule of thumb">
  Every sentence in a pitch should be one that couldn't have been said by a random company in your category. If it could, it's carrying no information, just costing capacity.
</Callout>

## Efficient coding matches source statistics

Morse gave "E" a dot and "Q" a long sequence because E is common. In a pitch, concepts the receiver already processes fluently (fiduciary duty, advice at scale, MiFID) deserve short codes — lean on them, don't re-explain. Rare, novel concepts (causal knowledge graph, temporal asset overlay, Mastra runtime) deserve the longer codes.

The common failure mode is the opposite: founders over-explain what the buyer already knows and under-explain the genuinely new thing. Swap the lengths.

## Redundancy fights noise, but has a budget

Shannon calculates English at ~50% redundant — which is what lets you still read a text with typos. Sales and fundraising channels are spectacularly noisy: the buyer is distracted, half-remembers the last call, has three other vendors in their head.

Redundancy across touchpoints (cold email → deck → meeting → follow-up, each carrying the same core message in a different encoding) is exactly right. Redundancy within a single message — saying the same thing three ways in one paragraph — burns capacity the receiver could have spent on a second point.

The craft is knowing which redundancy you're in.

## Channel capacity is real, and smaller than you think

A 30-minute investor meeting has a capacity of maybe two or three bits that survive to the partner meeting: [category], [one differentiator], [one proof point]. Shannon's coding theorem says that below capacity you can get arbitrarily high fidelity; above it, no amount of cleverness rescues you.

So the decision is which two or three bits you ship. Motif's current set — [B2B2C wealth intelligence], [causal graph, not RAG/sentiment], [traction + regulated path] — is a good set. Resist the urge to add a fourth.

## Equivocation is what to fix next

After a pitch, the receiver has residual uncertainty about what you actually are — Shannon calls this H_y(x). The second meeting's agenda should be whatever the equivocation was after the first, not a re-transmission of what already got through.

This is why a good investor-FAQ doc is so leveraged: it drives down equivocation for the items you know come up.

## The semantic content is "irrelevant"

Shannon's most provocative move is the claim that the semantic content is irrelevant to the engineering problem. That's uncomfortable for a craft-minded builder, because meaning is the whole point of the thing you're making.

But the right reading is: the meaning can be completely intact inside you and still not arrive. Sales and fundraise are not about having a good thing; they're the transmission layer for the good thing. Treating them as an engineering problem — sources, channels, capacity, noise, coding — is, ironically, the most respectful thing you can do to the underlying work.
