I built a B2C product. People use it. People like it. And still: **nobody is buying.**

Not because the product is broken. Because distribution is expensive, and B2B is slow.

<Divider />

## B2C is a budget game

Early-stage B2C has a cruel shape: you ship, people try it, feedback is goodâ€¦ then growth stalls.

That stall is **distribution**.

The advice you get is always the same: *"just go viral"*. But "go viral" is not a strategy. It's what happens when distribution is solved. And distribution usually costs money.

If you're bootstrapped or running on a tight runway, the honest version is:

<Callout type="warning" title="The real B2C strategy">
  Win the lottery â€” but make it a strategy.
</Callout>

## So I went B2B2C (selling the engine)

I didn't really pivot. The consumer app is still the product. I'm just trying to sell the underlying platform to companies who already have distribution.

**B2B2C is the pragmatic version of the dream:**

  <li><strong>The Platform</strong> â€” is the business</li>

## The new problem: nobody buys fast

Here's what I didn't fully appreciate: **B2B rarely says "no"**. It says:

<div className="bg-gray-50 dark:bg-gray-800/50 rounded-xl p-6 my-6 border-l-4 border-amber-500">
  <ul className="space-y-2 text-gray-600 dark:text-gray-300 italic">
    <li>"This is very interesting."</li>
    <li>"We should do a pilot."</li>
    <li>"Let's align internally."</li>
    <li>"Budget is next quarter."</li>
  </ul>
</div>

You get LOIs. You get champions. And still, nothing closes.

<div className="bg-gray-50 dark:bg-gray-800/50 rounded-xl p-6 my-6 border-l-4 border-amber-500 italic text-lg text-gray-700 dark:text-gray-200">
  LOIs are compliments. Contracts are oxygen.
</div>

Because B2B buying is not a moment. It's a process. And that process is designed to reduce risk â€” not to reward speed.

The uncomfortable answer is: it could be true. But it's usually not that simple.

## Technique: cold emails + systems

Cold outreach is still the humbling machine.

Most people don't reply. Some reply just to say "no". Some say "sounds cool" and disappear.

But outreach has one hidden benefit: **it forces clarity.**

You can't hide behind vague value propositions in a cold email. You have to pick a real target, a real pain, and a real reason they should care.
