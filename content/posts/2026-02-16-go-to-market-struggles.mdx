I built a B2C product. People use it. People like it. And still: **nobody is buying.**

Not because the product is broken. Mostly because distribution is expensive, and B2B buying moves like cold molasses.

This is not just one company issue for me. I see the same GTM friction across every company I am involved with right now, and honestly I am still not sure who has truly figured GTM out.

<Divider />

## B2C is a budget game

Early-stage B2C has a painful pattern: you ship, people try it, feedback is good, then growth stalls.

That stall is usually **distribution**.

The advice you hear is "just go viral." I hate that line.

Going viral is an outcome, not a plan. And most of the time, distribution costs real money.

If you are bootstrapped or running a tight runway, the honest strategy often looks like this:

## So I went B2B2C (selling the engine)

I did not really pivot. The consumer app is still core.

I am trying to sell the underlying platform to companies that already have distribution.

**B2B2C is the pragmatic version of the dream:**

## The new problem: nobody buys fast

The part I underestimated: **B2B rarely says "no"**. It says:

    <li>"We should do a pilot."</li>
    <li>"Let's align internally."</li>
    <li>"Budget is next quarter."</li>

You collect LOIs. You collect champions. You still do not get paid.

B2B buying is not one decision. It is a chain of risk checks.

## Technique: cold emails + systems

Cold outreach is still the most humbling machine I know.

Most people do not reply. Some reply with a polite no. Some say "sounds cool" and vanish.

But outreach does one good thing: it forces clarity.

In a cold email, I cannot hide behind abstract value props. I need a clear buyer, clear pain, and clear reason to care right now.

## What I am planning next: a roadshow

My next move is a focused roadshow with warm intros.

I am not expecting to walk out with signed enterprise contracts on the spot. That almost never happens. What I want is momentum with teeth:

- a paid PoC (best outcome)
- an LOI plus clear commercial path
- a dated next step with the actual decision-maker

If I can leave meetings with a real owner, real pain, and real timeline, that is a win. Contract comes later, after the usual security, legal, and procurement maze.

<Divider />

## Conclusion

Right now, product quality is not my main bottleneck.

**Distribution is.**

And in B2B, distribution means timing, budgets, internal politics, and patience I do not always have.
