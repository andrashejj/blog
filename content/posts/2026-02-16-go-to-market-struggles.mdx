Building at **NBK Labs** has taught me one thing: I can build products that work, but figuring out how to *actually* sell them is a moving target. 

Whether it was building `pierwallet.com`, `flippapp.ai`, or my latest run with `chatwithmotif.com`, the technical playbook is consistent. We ship B2C first. It‚Äôs the only way to hear the "raw" customer voice [catalinfetean.substack.com](https://catalinfetean.substack.com/p/the-founder-led-marketing-playbook). But B2C growth isn't free. So we pivot to the "pragmatic" dream: **B2B2C**. We strip the UI, package the underlying platform, and try to sell it to partners who already have the users we want.

## The "Distribution" Wall

In B2C, you hit the wall early. You launch on Product Hunt, you get the Twitter/X threads, you see the spike. Then... flatline. 

Unless you have a massive marketing budget or a viral loop that actually pays for itself, B2C is a plateau game. My realization: **The app is the proof, but the platform is the business.**

Selling to other businesses (B2B2C) should be the shortcut. You leverage their distribution to reach the end consumer [culturekey.io](https://culturekey.io/harnessing-the-power-of-b2b2c-a-blueprint-for-growing-your-business/). But I‚Äôm learning the hard way that "leveraging" someone else's audience means navigating someone else's bureaucracy.

## "Interesting" is not a check

I‚Äôve had over 25 high-level conversations recently. The feedback is soul-crushing because it‚Äôs so *nice*. 

In B2B, nobody tells you your baby is ugly. They tell you it‚Äôs "fascinating." They sign LOIs (Letters of Intent). They ask for pilots.

  An LOI is a compliment. A contract is oxygen. I have plenty of compliments; I‚Äôm still gasping for oxygen.

B2B buying isn't a decision; it's a marathon of tiny permissions. You aren't just selling a solution; you're trying to outrun the "Quarterly Budget Cycle."

### The 8-Step Gauntlet
Based on my current pipeline, here is what the "playbook" actually looks like once you jump into the trenches:

1.  **The Hook**: Cold email or intro. (High effort, low hit rate).
2.  **The Ego Boost**: The "This is perfect for us" call.
3.  **The Champion**: Finding one person who loves it but has no budget power.
4.  **The Demo Loop**: Showing it to three more people who weren't on the first call.
5.  **The Pilot Trap**: They want it for free to "prove value." (Beware: free pilots rarely convert).
6.  **The Security/Legal Wall**: Where my tiny startup meets their 40-page compliance doc.
7.  **The Procurement Void**: Where enthusiasm goes to die.
8.  **The Contract**: The only step that matters.

## Is it the product or the GTM?

Every founder hits this spiral. If B2C growth stalls and B2B contracts won't close, you start asking: *Is the product actually shit?*

Usually, it's not the tech. It‚Äôs the **urgency**.

If you're in the B2B2C space, you're a "nice to have" until you‚Äôre a "must have." I‚Äôm learning that speed in sales comes from three very specific things:
*   **A Massive Pain**: Not "better UX," but "saving $X per month."
*   **An Owner**: One person whose bonus depends on solving that pain.
*   **A Deadline**: A reason they need to buy *today*, not next quarter.

If any of these are missing, you‚Äôre just a consultant providing free education.
